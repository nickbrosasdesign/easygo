# Research Objective

My object of the interviews is to find themes and insights from caretakers, professionals, and/or family members of those who interact with those who have disabilities. 

**Also note**, this research project was originally done in 2018, when the researcher (me) had smaller UX research knowledge base. 

Another objective of this research project is to:
1. Refamiliarize myself with  the base research the project was originally based on
2. Review and reanalyze the research through the broader lens of established UX research principles I have learned since the original project.
3. Create more context to feed into Claude for iterating on the 2026 redesign project.

# Synthesis

## Executive Summary

Three interviews were conducted to understand how people experience and think about urban accessibility for those with disabilities, with a focus on Seattle sidewalks and the role of technology (especially phone apps) in navigating the city. All three participants had direct exposure to disability — as a parent/teacher, a nurse, and a volunteer/sibling — giving them grounded perspectives. Common themes emerged around uneven sidewalk conditions across neighborhoods, the absence of real-time infrastructure information, and a strong unmet need for apps that surface accessibility status proactively. Existing tools like Google Maps are used but perceived as accessibility-blind. Participants independently converged on a crowdsourced hazard-reporting feature as the most desired solution.

---

## Individual Interview Summaries

### Andrew (Interview #1 in original notes)
Andrew has assisted people with disabilities through volunteering, Special Olympics, and his brother (who has a disability). He has a broad, systems-level perspective: he sees Seattle as better than most cities but still inadequate. He highlighted the pedestrian/car danger interface and his brother's anxiety around crossing streets. His own tech use is Google Maps for routing and location lookup. He doesn't use accessibility features and doesn't see a personal need for them, but acknowledges the gap for others.
### Jane (Interview #3 in original notes)
Jane has first-hand experience with disability as a parent, teacher, and friend. Her primary focus is employment support programs for people with disabilities, which she finds helpful but unsustainable due to high staff turnover from low wages. On sidewalks, she noted wheelchair ramps in Capitol Hill as a positive but flagged obstructions (sandwich boards, outdoor dining, guy wires) and the danger these pose to vision-impaired pedestrians. She personally reported guiding a blind pedestrian who was drifting into traffic. She has minimal tech use for navigation and doesn't personally use accessibility apps, but identified Access Bus scheduling as a critical unmet need — the pickup window can be an hour long, creating safety and reliability issues.
### Amanda (Interview #2 in original notes)
Amanda is a nurse who works with wheelchair users. She recounted a story from a patient whose accessible routes were unpredictably blocked by root-damaged sidewalks and missing curb cuts. She also personally struggled to push a patient in a wheelchair over a 1.5-inch raised crack. Her key insight: there is no system to know in advance whether a route will be navigable. She uses Google Maps (including offline) for walking routes but has never sought accessibility features. Her most concrete idea: a Waze-style crowdsourced map where users flag and resolve accessibility hazards in real time.

## Key Themes

### Theme 1: Sidewalk Conditions Are Inconsistent and Unpredictable
**Prevalence:** 3 of 3 participants  
**Summary:** All participants noted that sidewalk quality varies significantly by neighborhood, and that there is no reliable way to know in advance whether a route will be navigable for someone with a mobility disability.  
**Supporting Evidence:**
- "They would get halfway down the sidewalk and discover that the sidewalk was all torn up from roots or something. Or they would get all the way to the end of a block and there wouldn't be a cut out." — Amanda
- "There's a lot of, um, unexpected things in sidewalks, like sandwich boards in the middle of sidewalks… and restaurants that all of a sudden have this outdoor seating area that takes up half the sidewalk." — Jane
- "I can see that if you were someone who had accessibility issues, or were like, moving around in a wheelchair, it might be hard to get around." — Andrew  

**Implication:** A tool that surfaces real-time route accessibility conditions would address a pervasive, felt pain point across all user groups.

---

### Theme 2: No Good Information System for Accessibility
**Prevalence:** 3 of 3 participants  
**Summary:** Participants consistently identified the absence of a reliable, up-to-date information layer for accessibility — whether for sidewalks, programs, or transit.  
**Supporting Evidence:**
- "There's really no good system or way to know when you're walking down the sidewalk whether or not you're going to encounter an issue." — Amanda
- "There's a lot, a lot to keep up on [regarding programs and agencies]… it changes constantly." — Jane
- "I think that in general, being in the United States, and being in Seattle… doesn't by any means mean that we have adequate services, or meet the needs that people with disabilities have." — Andrew  

**Implication:** EasyGo has an opportunity to be the canonical information layer for accessibility — not just navigation, but program discovery and real-time conditions.

---

### Theme 3: Existing Apps Are Accessibility-Blind
**Prevalence:** 3 of 3 participants  
**Summary:** All three participants use Google Maps for navigation but none have ever used or found accessibility-specific features. They perceive the existing tools as built for able-bodied users.  
**Supporting Evidence:**
- "I use Google Maps to look up the quickest route for walking. I don't think I've ever used any other app for walking aside from Google Maps." — Amanda
- "There's nothing about accessibility [in Google Maps]. Is that something you haven't looked for? I personally haven't needed to." — Amanda
- "I'm sure there are apps, you know, for knowing that buses are on time. I don't know which one." — Jane  

**Implication:** Google Maps is the baseline mental model for navigation. EasyGo should feel as simple as Google Maps but with an accessibility layer on top — not a separate niche tool.

---

### Theme 4: Crowdsourced Hazard Reporting as the Killer Feature
**Prevalence:** 2 of 3 explicitly; 1 implicitly  
**Summary:** Amanda directly proposed a Waze-style feature for marking and resolving sidewalk hazards. Jane's emphasis on unpredictability implied a similar need. This was the most concrete and unprompted product idea across interviews.  
**Supporting Evidence:**
- "I think it would be really cool if… we noticed an area that had a raised route thing, we could just mark it on the app… like ways you can mark that there's a cop on the side of the road or a traffic accident… somebody could walk by later if it was fixed and mark it as resolved." — Amanda
- "Unexpected things on sidewalks… obstructions, definitely, I've seen obstructions in sidewalks." — Jane  

**Implication:** A community-maintained hazard layer is the most desired and intuitive feature. It also creates a network effect: the more users, the more accurate the map.

---

### Theme 5: Transit Reliability Is a Critical Gap (Especially Access Bus)
**Prevalence:** 1 of 3 (but high severity)  
**Summary:** Jane raised the Access Bus as a specific, named pain point: its pick-up windows can span an hour, leaving disabled riders stranded and unsafe while waiting, or chronically late.  
**Supporting Evidence:**
- "Maybe like an app that has something to do with the access service would be really helpful. And there should be." — Jane  

**Implication:** Real-time Access Bus tracking or status notifications is a high-value, underserved feature opportunity — especially since this population has no alternative.

---

### Theme 6: Physical Safety at Pedestrian-Car Interfaces
**Prevalence:** 2 of 3 participants  
**Summary:** Both Jane and Andrew described danger at street crossings — a blind pedestrian drifting into traffic, and Andrew's brother being bumped by cars.  
**Supporting Evidence:**
- "He was not walking straight, and I could see that he was going out into the traffic." — Jane
- "My brother… has been bumped by cars before, and is pretty intimidated by crossing the street and just moving around." — Andrew  

**Implication:** Navigation safety at intersections — not just between them — is a real risk for users. Audio cues, safe crossing confirmation, or pedestrian signal integration could address this.

## UX Recommendations

**1. Build a crowdsourced hazard map (highest priority)**  
This was the most concrete, independently generated idea across interviews. Users understand the mental model immediately because they already use Waze. The feature should allow users to pin a hazard (raised crack, missing curb cut, blocked sidewalk), categorize it, and mark it resolved. Even a small network of users creates immediate value.

**2. Make Google Maps the reference point, not a competitor**  
All three participants use Google Maps. EasyGo should position itself as the accessibility layer that fills the gap Google Maps leaves — not a replacement navigation tool. Consider focusing on "I'm going somewhere — is this route accessible today?" rather than turn-by-turn navigation.

**3. Add Access Bus real-time status notifications**  
Jane identified this as a named, specific unmet need for a population with no alternatives. An integration with Metro Access or a similar transit API would address a high-severity, underserved problem.

**4. Design for caregivers and proxies, not just the person with the disability**  
All three interviewees are planning and navigating *on behalf of* someone else. EasyGo should support shared access, route planning for others, and the ability to monitor someone else's route.

**5. Recruit primary users for next research round**  
None of the current interviewees have a disability themselves. Their perspectives are valuable but filtered through proximity. Direct interviews with wheelchair users, vision-impaired navigators, and transit-dependent individuals with disabilities are essential before moving into design.

## Methodology Notes

Interviews were semi-structured, following a consistent script across participants. Participants were recruited from the interviewer's existing network and skewed toward people with indirect exposure to disability (caregivers, nurses, volunteers) rather than direct experience. This limits generalizability to primary users. Questions were open-ended with follow-up probes, and interviews were conducted in conversational settings. Transcripts were verbatim and have been lightly edited for readability in this document only.
